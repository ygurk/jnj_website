01

Name directions

Nine starting points across four flavors — founders' initials, plain / descriptive, slightly playful, and abstract / brandable. The right name should be easy to say on a phone call, easy to spell, and shouldn't make a non-technical owner feel they're hiring something complicated. The colored dot flags how easy each is likely to trademark and find a domain for.

Two Pines
Abstract
Two trees, two founders. Calm, rooted, a little local-and-handsome. Feels like a place, not a tech vendor — which reads as trustworthy and human.
"Pines" is common in business names; .com likely needs a qualifier (twopines.studio / trytwopines.com). Trademark: moderate.
Clearwork
Abstract
Coined compound that quietly promises "no jargon, no mess." Modern, confident, and the meaning is obvious without being literal. Strong all-rounder.
Coined word → easier to trademark and own. Domain likely gettable as clearwork.studio / clearwork.co.
Storefront
Descriptive
Speaks the client's language: a website is their digital storefront. Instantly tells a salon or contractor what you do for them. Warm, plain, on-the-nose in a good way.
Very common word → hard to trademark broadly, exact .com almost certainly taken. Best as "Storefront Studio" + a coined domain.
Good Company
Playful
A double meaning — a good business, and pleasant to be around. Friendly, memorable, disarming. Lowers the guard of a cautious owner immediately.
Common phrase; trademark in web-services class is plausible but crowded. Domain needs a twist (goodcompany.studio).
Mainstay
Abstract
"The thing you rely on." Signals steadiness and being there after launch — exactly the post-handoff worry small businesses have. Grown-up without being stuffy.
Real word, used elsewhere (incl. a senior-living brand) → check class overlap. Domain likely needs mainstay.studio / mainstayweb.com.
Handbuilt
Playful
Counters the "is this just a template?" fear. Suggests care, craft, made-for-you. Pairs beautifully with a warm palette and a contractor/maker clientele.
Descriptive-ish word; trademark moderate with a distinctive logo. handbuilt.studio / handbuilt.co likely available.
Foundry Lane
Abstract
"Foundry" = things get made; "Lane" = a friendly, local street. The pairing feels like a small shop with real craft. Brandable and easy to build a visual world around.
Two-word coinage → good trademark distinctiveness. Exact domain availability decent (foundrylane.com / .studio).
[A]&[B] Studio
Initials
e.g. "R&M Studio." The "&" makes the two-person partnership the whole story — you're buying real people, not a faceless agency. Classic, credible, ages well.
Initials are easy to clear & own once paired with "Studio/Web." Domain usually gettable. Tell me your initials and I'll set it.
Bright Corner
Playful
Optimistic and neighborly — "the bright corner of the internet that's yours." Approachable first, with enough warmth to feel like a partner rather than a contractor.
Common words but distinctive together; trademark moderate. Domain likely needs a tweak (brightcorner.studio).
02

Color directions

Color does a lot of the first-impression work — people form a snap judgment about a brand in seconds, and a big chunk of that is driven by color before they read a single word. Here are three honest, distinct worlds. Think of them as head, heart, and growth. Each lists a primary, a secondary, an accent (for buttons / calls-to-action), and a background neutral.

#1E4E8C #2E6FB8 #F2994A #F6F8FB
Steady Blue
The "head" — safe & credible

Blue is the most universally trusted color — it's why banks, insurers, and tech lean on it. We warm it with a friendly amber accent and a clean, soft background so it reads capable, not cold or corporate.

Primary#1E4E8C — text, links
Secondary#2E6FB8 — buttons, UI
Accent#F2994A — CTAs, highlights
Background#F6F8FB on #16202E ink
Feels like: Stripe / PayPal trust, softened. Watch-out: blue is everywhere — the warm accent is what keeps it from feeling generic.
#BB5A3C #6E8C6A #E0A33E #FBF5EC
Warm Clay
The "heart" — human & local

Terracotta, sage, and cream feel handmade and neighborly — like a brand that actually cares about your shop. This is the most approachable direction and the hardest for a competitor to feel cold next to.

Primary#BB5A3C — clay / brand
Secondary#6E8C6A — sage support
Accent#E0A33E — CTAs, warmth
Background#FBF5EC on #2E2620 ink
Feels like: Mailchimp / Basecamp friendliness, Airbnb warmth. Watch-out: pair with crisp type so "warm" never tips into "soft / less technical."
#1F4D3D #2E6B52 #E7B24C #F6F4EE
Confident Green
The "growth" — established & premium

Deep forest green signals growth, stability, and money-well-spent without shouting. With a warm gold accent it feels premium but grounded — the most "we've been doing this a while" of the three.

Primary#1F4D3D — deep pine
Secondary#2E6B52 — UI green
Accent#E7B24C — CTAs, gold
Background#F6F4EE on #16241D ink
Feels like: a modern fintech (Wise-ish) meets a calm wellness brand. Watch-out: keep it warm — pure green can drift "eco / clinical" if the gold is dropped.

Every primary above is dark enough to pass WCAG AA contrast for text on its light background; the bright accents are reserved for buttons and small highlights (not body text) so calls-to-action always pop and nothing important gets hard to read. I'll lock exact, accessibility-checked pairings into the system once you choose.

04

Logo approaches

Three ways to build the mark — each needs to survive being a website header, a tiny favicon, a business card, and a round social avatar. These are rough concept sketches to react to, not finished art; once we pick a name + palette I'll draw them properly. (Marks below use placeholder names from Section 01.)

Concept
Clearwork.

1 · Wordmark only

Just the name, set with care and one small custom detail (here, a colored period — a tiny "full stop, done right"). Most flexible and the cheapest to use everywhere. Restraint reads as confidence; a warm typeface keeps it friendly. Favicon = the first letter.

Concept
R&M

2 · Lettermark + container

Your initials in a soft rounded badge. The "&" makes the two-person partnership the hero — clients love knowing they're hiring real people. Perfect as a favicon and round avatar, since it's built to be small and square.

Concept
Foundry Lane

3 · Abstract symbol + wordmark

A simple geometric mark beside the name — here two overlapping blocks (two founders, building together / a window onto your site). Most "real brand," most memorable, scales to a favicon on its own. Reads as designed and competent while the metaphor stays human.
